# The Ad Manual

### How to Write Ads That Make People Stop, Click, and Buy

*Principles extracted from direct response copywriting masterclass. Adapted into an operational manual.*

---

## HOW TO USE THIS MANUAL

This manual is built in the order you should work: research first, then message, then execution.

Don't skip to the formulas. The formulas are the last 20%. The first 80% is knowing what to say. If you say the right thing badly, you'll still make money. If you say the wrong thing beautifully, you'll go broke.

---

## PART 1: BEFORE YOU WRITE A SINGLE WORD

### 1.1 — The Only Job of an Ad

Every step of your funnel has one specific job. Most people think the ad is supposed to sell the product. It's not.

The prospect sees your ad on Facebook, Instagram, Google — they can't buy from the ad. So why try to sell them in the ad?

**The ad's only job is to sell the click.** Get them to click and go to the landing page. The landing page's job is to generate the lead or get the sale.

If you confuse these jobs, everything breaks. Your ad tries to do too much, your prospect feels overwhelmed, and they scroll past.

When you sit down to write an ad, ask yourself one question: *"What do I need to say to make this person click?"* — not "What do I need to say to make them buy?"

### 1.2 — Your Prospect Only Cares About Themselves

Your business and your product is actually a barrier between your prospect and what they want. If they had a choice, they would just get the outcome without having to deal with you. They just want the outcome.

When you write copy, the only thing you should be thinking about is your prospect and what they want. Don't think about how long you've been in business, how big your team is. They don't care.

Think about what's keeping your prospect up at night at 3:00 a.m. tossing and turning in a cold sweat. What are their pains and fears, their hopes and dreams?

**The 80/20 test:** Once you've written a piece of copy, look at it and ask — is 80% of this talking about the prospect and the big benefit they want, and only 10-20% talking about me or how I deliver the outcome?

A telltale sign of bad copy: the words "and," "we," and "I" appear more often than "you."

### 1.3 — Features Tell, Benefits Sell

Another sin: listing all the features and bells and whistles without focusing on the benefits.

They don't want to know it's a 12-week fitness program with calculated macros and 6:00 a.m. workouts. They want to know about dropping dress sizes, getting six-pack abs, and feeling the best they ever have.

If you focus on the features — waking up early, grueling workouts, counting calories — they won't even want to do it because you're selling them work. People don't want work. They want the outcome.

**The rule:** Always focus on the desired benefit someone will get from your product. Why are they buying it? They're not buying to buy it. They're buying to get a benefit.

### 1.4 — Human Psychology Doesn't Change

The same principles that work today were working 100, 200, 300, even 500 years ago. The technology changes — Google ads, TikTok ads, all of the platforms will change and those algorithms will forever change. But the thing that doesn't change is human psychology.

Your ads are an army of little salespeople that you're deploying out there, and their sole job is to go capture money and bring more of that money back home.

## PART 2: RESEARCH — THE GAME IS WON HERE

### 2.1 — What to Say vs. How to Say It

Once somebody reads their first book on copywriting, they obsess over *how* to say things and not *what* to say. But what you say is infinitely more important than how you say it.

In every single market there is a bullseye — if you hit it, your prospects buy emotionally, irrationally, and in huge quantities. It's not about how you say things — it's about what you say.

You can't have a shitty message written in the best way beat a not-so-good written message that's saying the right thing.

Only once you know you're saying the right thing do you obsess over the best ways to say it.

### 2.2 — Forum Foraging: How to Find What to Say

Go on the internet — Facebook groups, forums, Reddit — and be a fly on the wall. Obsess over your prospect. What are the questions? What's keeping them up at night? What are their concerns?

Open up their emotions — frustrations, considerations. Copy all of those comments about their pains and fears, hopes and dreams into a document (the **Halo Strategy** document).

When it comes time to write your copy, you're not sitting down with a blank page trying to craft a compelling message. Instead, you're **assembling** the copy — taking all of the pains and fears you've already observed and assembling them using a framework into a pitch.

**Where to mine this gold:**

- Facebook groups (related to your niche)
- Google reviews (your product AND competitors)
- YouTube comments (on videos your prospect watches)
- Reddit / subreddits (where your prospect asks questions)
- Competitor reviews (5-star, 1-star, AND 3-star — all tell different stories)

Don't spend 5-10 minutes on this. Spend a day. Spend multiple days. If you do all of this, you will have a document absolutely stacked to the brim with marketing gold.

### 2.3 — Sound Like One of Them

You want to sound like one of them. The only way to do that is to obsess and study these people, get their exact vernacular and language, and put that into a message where it's like: "This person must be one of us because they know exactly my pain and they can articulate it better than I can myself."

When your prospect reads that, they don't think "this is good marketing." They think: "This person understands me." And therefore they assume you have the solution.

This is Robert Collier's principle: enter the conversation that is already taking place in your prospect's mind. You don't want to create desire. You want to **channel** desire. The way you channel it is by entering that conversation and simply channeling it down to your product or service.

### 2.4 — Why Founders Fall Out of Touch

Most founders think they know their prospect. They don't. Markets shift. Founders have a jaded, expert-level view of the market. Once they hire layers of organization, they get removed from the front lines — no longer speaking to prospects on sales calls, no longer looking at customer service. They fall out of touch.

This research is not a once-and-done exercise. You can't go more than 6 months without doing it.

### 2.5 — Testing Angles, Not Words

You figure out what to say by testing multiple different *angles* — not trying to say the same thing in different ways, but testing different things entirely.

You'll notice that some of your ads have an outlier that resonates better than everything else. That's when you've identified what to say. Then, and only then, do you start optimizing how you say it.

## PART 3: THE "UNDER THE FINGERNAIL" PRINCIPLE

### 3.1 — The Shift That Changes Everything

When you start writing ads that focus all your efforts on describing the pain your prospect is experiencing in vivid, vivid detail — so specific they can't deny it — and then focus on the big benefit they want more than anything, things go crazy.

This is "under the fingernail" copy. It's so specific that they can't deny it. That's when your words strike like lightning.

### 3.2 — How to Apply It

The structure is simple:

1. **Describe their pain with surgical precision.** Use their exact words. Their exact situations. The specific moment at 2 a.m. when the thought hits them.

2. **Name the alternatives they've tried and why those failed.** Show you understand their full journey, not just the current moment.

3. **Paint the desired outcome in vivid detail.** What does it look like, what does it smell like, how shiny is it? Show them the other side.

4. **Bridge the gap.** "Let me show you how to get from where you are right now to exactly where you want to go."

When a prospect sees that, it's like a breath of fresh air. Every other ad is talking about the business, the features, the years of experience. Nothing speaks to their desired end state.

### 3.3 — Record Your Red-Hot Pitch

The first part of your process should be to record yourself (or your best salesperson) giving a red-hot pitch. Sit down in front of a prospect and go through all their questions, concerns, pains and fears, hopes and dreams, then transition into a pitch. Let's say it goes 30-40 minutes.

That's effectively what your sales message must do — one to many instead of one to one.

After you record that pitch, look at your landing page and ads. If they're saying something different, light them on fire.

**Copywriting is just salesmanship multiplied.** The further you get away from that, the worse your ads convert.

## PART 4: HOW TO SELL THE CLICK

### 4.1 — What Every Ad Needs

The ad needs three things:

1. **A big, specific benefit** that calls out your audience
2. **Hyper-specificity** — the more your market has been advertised to, the more specific you need to be
3. **An element of intrigue** — if there's no intrigue, no one clicks

You can't just say "Here's how to lose weight." You need something like: "This weird 2-minute exercise 45 minutes before bedtime puts your body in a state where you build muscle automatically."

Think of your market as a raging river. Your job is to channel some of that desire to your product by hijacking their attention. Your prospect is a sleepwalking zombie. It's freezing cold outside and you need to convince them to get up and run around the block.

### 4.2 — What Drives a Click

- **Curiosity** — the biggest driver, but must be specific to your market, not blind curiosity
- **Shock** — a pattern interrupt that breaks the scroll
- **Implied benefit** — promising they'll get a benefit by clicking
- **Direct benefit** — crystal clear statement of what they'll get
- **Vanity, fear, self-interest** — becoming richer, fitter, more attractive
- **All layered with curiosity** — every element above works better when wrapped in curiosity

### 4.3 — The Specificity Rule

You can't say "grow your business." You need: "Here's how to get 150 to 250 highly qualified leads each and every month on autopilot."

You can't say "lose weight." You need: "Here's how to lose 12-15 kgs in the next 9 weeks without eating like a rabbit or doing endless cardio."

The more jaded your market — the more they've been advertised to — the deeper the specificity needs to go.

## PART 5: HEADLINES

### 5.1 — Why Headlines Are Everything

In 1982, Norah Hayden wrote a book called *Astrological Love*. It bombed — sold about 2,000 copies, the rest were dumped at dollar stores. A publisher bought the rights and republished it without changing a single word inside. Just the title and cover. New title: *How to Satisfy a Woman Every Time and Have Her Beg for More*. Result: 2.3 million copies in the first 18 months. New York Times bestseller.

Same book. Same content. Different packaging.

As David Ogilvy said: once you've written your headline, you've already spent 80 cents of your dollar. Five times more people will read the headline than the rest of your body copy. Spend 80% of your time on the headline.

### 5.2 — Where to Study Headlines

Don't just study classic copywriters — they didn't compete with the chaotic blizzard of the internet. Study where the best headline writers are today: follow the money and the attention.

TMZ, New York Times, Lad Bible, social media sites, gossip sites. They are masters at hijacking attention. They use: breaking news, "exposed," numbers, shocking images, 3-4 gripping words. Words like: exposed, shocked, leak, "wild details emerge." They elicit an emotion in the first 3 seconds.

You're not just competing with your local competitor anymore. You're on the internet, competing with everything on your prospect's news feed.

Don't reinvent the wheel — swipe and deploy, making it applicable to your market.

### 5.3 — The 5 Headline Essentials

**1. It Must Grab Them.** It doesn't need to tell the full story. It needs to grab your prospect by the throat and demand their attention. Most headlines should be 3-4 words — a grabber. The headline's job is nothing more than to grab attention.

**2. Always Use Numbers.** Numbers give prospects a tangible object to wrap their mind around. That's why listicles work. "The 7 things no financial planner would dare tell you" — you immediately want to know what those 7 things are. Every time in blind split tests, headlines with numbers outperform those without.

**3. Have Burning Curiosity.** Load it with curiosity. Use buzzwords: shocking, alarming, breaking news. Add a twist/qualifier: "7 things no property investor would dare tell you." Address the biggest objection: "How to start an online business, even if you're a complete beginner and have absolutely no idea where to start."

**4. Show What's In It For Them.** Every ad needs a big benefit. You can catch more flies with honey than vinegar. Always promise a big, specific benefit — that answers "What's in it for me?" Talk more about them than about you.

**5. Make Them Specific.** The more jaded your market, the deeper the specificity needed.

### 5.4 — Headline Formulas

**Formula 1:** X ways to achieve [desirable thing] without doing [undesirable thing]
> *"6 ways to get washboard abs without doing a single sit-up"*

**Formula 2:** Do [difficult thing] in [specific period of time], even if [shortcomings]
> *"Pay off your mortgage in 7 years even if you have a modest income"*

**Formula 3:** Achieve [desired thing] like an expert, even without [something expected]
> *"Paint a masterpiece like Picasso without ever having a single lesson"*

**Formula 4:** How to eliminate [biggest problem] without [the thing they hate] within [specific time frame]
> *"How to eliminate your muffin top without giving up the foods you love within 21 days"*

## PART 6: BULLET COPY / FASCINATIONS

### 6.1 — The Secret Weapon

These little teaser nuggets of copy need to be hyper-specific and create enough intrigue that someone would buy off one bullet alone. Each bullet should justify the purchase all by itself.

Bullet copy is often one of the most-read parts of a sales message because it's scannable. People read the headline, sub-headline, scroll to the bottom, check the price and CTA, scroll back up, read sub-headlines, then spend disproportionate time reading bullets.

Don't use bullets to make specific claims. Don't barrage and sell. **Play with them. Tease them.** Dial curiosity up to 10/10.

As Claude Hopkins said: "Curiosity is among the strongest human incentives."

### 6.2 — Fascination Formulas

**1. "You need X, right? Wrong."** — Address a common belief and talk against it.
> *"You need to eat at a calorie deficit to lose weight. Wrong."*

**2. "X ways to [why]"**
> *"5 ways to meet single women in Melbourne"*

**3. "Where to find [why]"**
> *"Where to find the most ravenous, hyperactive buyers online"*

**4. "How to eliminate X"**
> *"How to stop joint pain forever"*

**5. "What you should never do"** — People are more compelled to know what NOT to do (fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain).

**6. "Say goodbye to X frustration"**
> *"Say goodbye to calorie counting and hour-long cardio sessions"*

**7. "The truth about [why]"**
> *"The dirty truth about fish oil revealed"*
> *"The dirty truth about property investment that no one would dare tell you"*

## PART 7: OVERCOMING SKEPTICISM

### 7.1 — The Reality

Skepticism is at an all-time high. People have never been more jaded. Barriers to entry for starting a business have never been lower, so you've got unqualified people selling to customers, delivering bad experiences, and driving skepticism through the roof.

### 7.2 — Sell Against the Alternatives

You need to clearly communicate that you understand all the other alternatives they've tried. Then highlight why those alternatives failed them.

The question plaguing your prospect's mind is: **"How is this different?"**

A buyer is a buyer is a buyer. The people buying from you are buying from everybody else. Understand what they're buying so you can sell against those things.

**Example structure:**

> "You've probably tried [alternative 1], [alternative 2], [alternative 3] — and none of it works. You [specific failure pattern]. It's because they don't address the root problem: [root cause]. [Your product] solves that specific issue."

### 7.3 — The Antidote to Skepticism Is Value

Your ads and funnels need to be valuable in themselves. Prove you can help people by actually helping them. Unpack a bit of your product. Give them a short bit of the results in the ad itself.

When you do that, the prospect thinks: "If this person is giving this away for free, imagine what their paid stuff is like."

**Make your free stuff better than your competitor's paid stuff.** When you do that, you take over the market.

### 7.4 — Handle Objections Before They Come Up

After writing your sales message, it should sound so compelling that only a lunatic wouldn't buy. If people still aren't buying, the answer is usually: they don't believe you.

Handle objections directly in the copy before they come up. Highlight: "This person had this problem, tried this, but once they tried your thing, they got all these benefits." As they read, they're nodding: "Yes, that makes sense." All objections handled. That's when people pull out their wallets.

## PART 8: WRITING RULES

### 8.1 — Write at a Sixth Grade Reading Level

The New York Times best-selling authors, the biggest YouTubers in the world, and people appointed to the most powerful offices in the world all speak and write at a sixth grade reading level or below.

You will not alienate people by making your message too clear, but you will by making it too confusing. Speak to the lowest common denominator. You don't want them to notice your writing — you want them to be moved by your message.

**Use the Hemingway App.** Paste your copy in and it will show you the reading level and highlight sentences that are too difficult. Go through and eliminate every flag. That's when your copy becomes smooth — people slip their way down the page.

Consumption precedes conversion. The best way to get people to consume your message is by writing at a sixth grade reading level.

If you do nothing else, take an ad or landing page and bring the reading level from 9-12 down to 6. You will see conversions shoot through the roof.

### 8.2 — Writing Rhythm

Use simple words, short sentences. Two short sentences followed by a long one gives your writing rhythm and cadence.

**Clarity beats cleverness every single time.**

### 8.3 — Writing Is Thinking

A good writer is a good thinker. If you can't communicate it in your mind, you'll never be clear when writing. A lot of the writing isn't done at your keyboard — it's done on long walks, when you're really thinking about the problem and the messaging becomes clear.

### 8.4 — Long Form vs. Short Form

Only morons believe short-form copy will outperform long-form copy — with one caveat: you can't have copy that's long for the sake of being long.

Someone who's not going to read long copy is also not going to read short copy. A buyer reads all the copy. They want every objection handled, every question answered.

**But edit ruthlessly.** Even though long-form converts better, after writing, the first thing you do is edit and cut. Get it all out on paper, then ruthlessly edit. Every single word needs to fight for its right to be on the page. Each word must move the prospect one step closer to buying. It can't ramble.

**Make it "read or die."** It must be so interesting the reader feels like they *have* to keep reading. Use simple words, short sentences, but also wink at your prospect and add elements of entertainment. Make it fun — not a task.

**Who reads all this copy?** The buyers. The people who have questions that need answering. They've tried multiple other products. They read every word, the Q&A, the reviews — they're scouring the depths of the internet.

Don't show your copy to friends who aren't in the market. Show it to actual prospects.

## PART 9: THE AD CREATION PROCESS — STEP BY STEP

### Step 1: Research (Days, Not Minutes)

Open your Halo Strategy document. Fill it with:

- Exact phrases from forums, Reddit, reviews, YouTube comments
- Pains and fears (what keeps them up at 3 a.m.)
- Hopes and dreams (what does the other side look like)
- Failed alternatives they've tried
- Objections they voice before buying
- The exact vernacular they use — not your version of it

### Step 2: Identify the Angle

Pick one specific pain point or desire from your research. This is the angle. Don't try to say everything in one ad. One ad = one angle.

Test multiple angles. Don't try to say the same thing in different ways — test different things entirely. Find the outlier.

### Step 3: Write the Hook (Headline)

Use the 5 Headline Essentials (grab them, use numbers, burning curiosity, what's in it for them, specificity). Use the formulas if you're stuck.

Spend 80% of your creative time here. The hook determines whether anything else gets read.

### Step 4: Describe Their Pain (Under the Fingernail)

2-4 sentences describing their current situation so vividly they feel seen. Use their words, not yours.

### Step 5: Agitate + Name Failed Alternatives

"You've probably tried X, Y, Z. None of it worked because [root cause]."

### Step 6: Paint the Outcome

What does the other side look like? Be specific. Sensory. Emotional.

### Step 7: Bridge to the Click

"That's why we built this." or "Here's how to get there." Give them one clear reason to click and one clear action.

### Step 8: CTA

One clear call to action. For quiz funnels: "Take the Quiz" outperforms "Learn More" by 20-40%. For direct sale: "Download" beats "Shop Now" when selling a digital product.

### Step 9: Check

- Is 80%+ about the prospect?
- Is the reading level at grade 6?
- Does every sentence fight for its place?
- Would I click this if I were them?
- Is there one clear action to take?

## PART 10: QUICK REFERENCE — THE NON-NEGOTIABLES

1. **The ad sells the click. The page sells the product.** Never confuse these jobs.
2. **What you say matters more than how you say it.** Research before writing. Always.
3. **Your prospect only cares about themselves.** 80% about them, 20% about you. Maximum.
4. **Features tell, benefits sell.** Sell the outcome, not the work.
5. **Under the fingernail.** Describe their pain so specifically they can't deny it.
6. **Sixth grade reading level.** Clarity beats cleverness.
7. **Sell against the alternatives.** They've tried other things. Acknowledge that.
8. **Make your free stuff better than your competitor's paid stuff.** Value kills skepticism.
9. **Every word fights for its right to be on the page.** Edit ruthlessly.
10. **Test angles, not words.** Find what to say before optimizing how to say it.
11. **Spend 80% of your time on the headline.** It determines whether anything else gets read.
12. **Don't create desire — channel it.** Enter the conversation already happening in their head.
