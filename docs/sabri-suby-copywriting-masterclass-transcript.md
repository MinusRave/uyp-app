## 2. What Is Copywriting?

- **Branded copywriting** — writing cute jingles and things that make people giggle.

The thing that I have specialized in is direct response, which is how to write black words on a white page and actually move someone emotionally to the point where they will do whatever it is that you're asking them to do.

It's all about making a very clear and compelling message that gets people to do whatever it is that you want them to do — whether you want them to apply for a job, motivate a team, get a lead to raise their hand, or get a complete stranger on the internet to pull out their credit card without ever meeting you.

## 6. Core Principle: Your Prospect Only Cares About Themselves

Your prospect only cares about themselves. Most people when they're writing copy think, "I need to write all the things about my company, about the products and services that I sell." But no one gives a flying f*** about any of that.

Your business and your product is actually a barrier between your prospect getting what they want. If they had a choice, they would just get the outcome without having to deal with you. They just want the outcome.

When you write copy, the only thing you should be thinking about is your prospect and what they want. Don't think about how long you've been in business, how big your team is. They don't care. They just want the outcome.

Think about what's keeping your prospect up at night at 3:00 a.m. tossing and turning in a cold sweat. What are their pains and fears, their hopes and dreams?

### The Breakthrough at 17

I had this breakthrough when I was 17, reading off a script. The script was front-heavy on me introducing who I was and the company I was calling from. For the first 10–15 seconds, the prospect was just trying to figure out who I was and why I was calling.

Instead of calling them up and introducing the company, I would just catch their first name and go straight into the problem. "Oh, hey Bob. It's Sabri here calling up from XYZ" — and then straight into the problem we solve.

By making that one change, I went from being the worst salesperson to becoming the best, literally in a matter of a week.

### The 80/20 Test

One test I still do today: once I've written a piece of copy, I look at it and ask — is 80% of this talking about the prospect and the big benefit they want, and only 10–20% talking about me or how I deliver the outcome? It needs to be 80% focused on the prospect, their pain, and where they're trying to go.

## 7. Features Tell, Benefits Sell

Another sin people make: they list out all the features and bells and whistles and don't focus on the benefits. Features tell, and benefits sell.

They don't want to know it's a 12-week fitness program with calculated macros and 6:00 a.m. workouts. They want to know about dropping dress sizes, getting six-pack abs, and feeling the best they ever have.

If you focused on the features — waking up early, grueling workouts, counting calories — they won't even want to do it because you're selling them work. People don't want work. They want the outcome.

Always focus on the desired benefit someone will get from your product. Why are they buying it? They're not buying to buy it. They're buying to get a benefit.

## 9. What to Say vs. How to Say It

Once somebody reads their first book on copywriting, what I observe is they obsess over *how* to say things and not *what* to say. But what you say is infinitely more important than how you say it.

In every single market there is a bullseye that if you hit it, your prospects buy emotionally, irrationally, and in huge quantities. It's not about how you say things — it's about what you say.

### Forum Foraging: How to Find What to Say

The best way I've figured out what to say is by doing what I call **forum foraging** — going on the internet, into Facebook groups, forums, Reddit, and being a fly on the wall. Obsessing over your prospect. What are the questions? What's keeping them up at night? What are their concerns?

Open up their emotions — frustrations, considerations. Copy all of those comments about their pains and fears, hopes and dreams into a document (I call it the **Halo Strategy**).

When it comes time to write your copy, you're not sitting down with a blank page trying to craft a compelling message. Instead, you're **assembling** the copy — taking all of the pains and fears you've already observed and assembling them using a framework into a pitch.

You want to sound like one of them. The only way to do that is to obsess and study these people, get their exact vernacular and language, and put that into a message where it's like, "This person must be one of us because they know exactly my pain and they can articulate it better than I can myself." Therefore they assume you have the solution.

### Where to Mine This Gold

- Facebook groups
- Google reviews
- YouTube comments
- Reddit / subreddits
- Chrome extension called "Discussions" (adds a forums-only tab in Google Search)
- Competitor reviews (5-star, 1-star, 3-star)

Don't spend 5–10 minutes on this. Spend a day. Spend multiple days. If you do all of this, you will have a document absolutely stacked to the brim with marketing gold.

### Why Founders Fall Out of Touch

Most founders think they know their prospect. They don't. Markets shift. Founders have a jaded, expert-level view of the market. Once they hire layers of organization, they get removed from the front lines — no longer speaking to prospects on sales calls, no longer looking at customer service. They fall out of touch.

This is not a once-and-done exercise. You can't go more than 6 months without doing this. Thanks to AI and how technology is advancing, there's a massive shift every week.

### Enter the Conversation Already Happening

This allows you to enter the conversation that is already taking place in your prospect's mind — as Robert Collier (a copywriter from hundreds of years ago) said. You don't want to create desire. You want to **channel** desire. The way you channel it is by entering that conversation and simply channeling it down to your product or service.

When you write words that are so specific, in the exact vernacular your market speaks in, it gets under their fingernails. It's so specific that they can't deny it. That's when your words strike like lightning.

### Testing What to Say: Find the Outlier

Only once you know you're saying the right thing do you obsess over the best ways to say it. You figure that out by testing multiple different *angles* — not trying to say the same thing in different ways, but testing different things entirely. You'll notice that some of your ads have an outlier that resonates better than everything else. That's when you've identified what to say.

The game is won in the research. That's not the sexy thing, but if you look at the most successful marketing campaigns ever, behind them was a copywriter obsessed with research.

You can't have a shitty message written in the best way beat a not-so-good written message that's saying the right thing.

## 10. Write at a Sixth Grade Reading Level

I've studied the New York Times best-selling authors, the biggest YouTubers in the world, and people appointed to the most powerful offices in the world. They all speak and write at a sixth grade reading level or below.

You will not alienate people by making your message too clear, but you will by making it too confusing. Speak to the lowest common denominator of your market. You don't want them to notice your writing — you want them to be moved by your message.

### The Hemingway App

Use the **Hemingway App**. They have a free version through a web browser, or you can buy the desktop version for about $19. Paste your copy in and it will:
- Show you the reading level (Flesch-Kincaid score)
- Highlight sentences that are difficult to read (red and yellow)

Go through and eliminate every flag. That's when your copy becomes as smooth as butter — people slip their way down the page. Consumption precedes conversion. The best way to get people to consume your message is by writing at a sixth grade reading level.

If you do nothing else, take an ad or landing page and bring the reading level from 9–12 down to 6. You will see conversions shoot through the roof.

### Writing Is Thinking

A good writer is a good thinker. If you can't communicate it in your mind, you'll never be clear when writing. A lot of the writing isn't done at your keyboard — it's done on long walks, when you're really thinking about the problem and the messaging becomes clear.

Use simple words, short sentences. Short, punchy sentences. Two short sentences followed by a long one gives your writing rhythm and cadence. **Clarity beats cleverness every single time.**

## 11. The Biggest Mistake: Making It About You

If you look at most people's websites: "We've been in business for 17 years, our team is so big, we've got all this experience." It's just a declaration to why they exist — not about the prospect.

Do a search in any industry and you'll see: "The trusted experts, been in business for 30 years, our team is so experienced." Generic, watered-down messaging that doesn't get anyone to buy. 99% of businesses have nothing resembling good copy. It's so easy to stand out because everyone's just talking about themselves.

A telltale sign: they say "and," "we," and "I" more than they say "you."

From the prospect's shoes — they search Google, they see a coliseum of competitors all shouting about how many years they've been in business. How do they differentiate?

If you can just come out and say: "Hey, I know you're experiencing this. You've probably tried X, Y, and Z, but you still haven't got the outcome. I bet you want to get here — this is exactly what it looks like, what it smells like, how shiny it is. Let me show you how to get from where you are right now to exactly where you want to go."

When a prospect sees that, it's like a breath of fresh air. Every other ad says the same thing. Nothing speaks to their desired end state.

## 12. The Fix: Record Your Red-Hot Pitch

Here is the fix. The very first part of your process should be to whip out your phone and record yourself (or your best salesperson) giving a **red-hot pitch**. Sit down in front of a prospect and go through all their questions, concerns, pains and fears, hopes and dreams, then transition into a pitch. Let's say it goes 30–40 minutes.

That's effectively what your sales message must do — one to many instead of one to one.

After you record that pitch, look at your landing page and ads. If they're saying something different, light them on fire. If they're pulling in leads by some miracle, they'd be doing it at 10% of what they could be.

**Copywriting is just salesmanship multiplied.** The further you get away from that, the worse your ads convert.

## 13. Every Step of the Funnel Has a Purpose

Every step of the funnel has a specific purpose. Most people think every part — the ad, the landing page — is meant to sell somebody. But it's not.

For most ads, people can't buy directly. They see a Google ad, they can't buy. They see a Facebook ad, they can't buy. So why try to sell them in the ad?

**The ad's job is to sell the click.** Get them to click and go to the landing page. The landing page's job is to generate the lead or get the sale.

### How to Sell the Click

The ad needs:
1. **A big, specific benefit** that calls out your audience
2. **Hyper-specificity** — the more your market has been advertised to, the more specific you need to be
3. **An element of intrigue** — if there's no intrigue, no one clicks

You can't just say "Here's how to lose weight." You need something like: "This weird berry grown in the Himalayas turns your body into a fat-burning furnace 24/7" or "This weird 2-minute exercise 45 minutes before bedtime puts your body in a state where you build muscle automatically."

Think of your market as a raging river. Your job is to channel some of that desire to your product by hijacking their attention. Your prospect is like a sleepwalking zombie — some fat, lethargic person sleeping on a couch covered in Doritos. It's freezing cold outside and you need to convince them to get up and run around the block.

### What Drives a Click?

- **Curiosity** (the biggest driver — but must be specific to your market, not blind curiosity)
- **Shock** — a pattern interrupt
- **Implied benefit** — promising they'll get a benefit by clicking
- **Direct benefit** — crystal clear statement of what they'll get
- **Vanity, fear, self-interest** — becoming richer, fitter, more attractive
- All layered with curiosity

## 14. Overcoming Skepticism in Copy

Skepticism is at an all-time high. People have never been more jaded. Barriers to entry for starting a business have never been lower, so you've got unqualified people selling to customers, delivering bad experiences, and driving skepticism up.

You need to clearly communicate that you understand all the other alternatives they've tried. Then highlight why those alternatives failed them.

### Example: Weight Loss

You can't just say "lose weight" anymore. You need to throw stones at all the other alternatives:

> "You've probably tried paleo, intermittent fasting, keto — and none of it works. You yo-yo diet, lose a few pounds, then put on more weight than when you began. It's because they don't address the root problem: your hormones. You have a specific issue with your thyroid that's messing up your hormones, making it impossible to lose weight no matter how much you work out or what you eat. I've created this special solution that helps you solve that."

You need to sell the way the market wants to buy.

### The Antidote to Skepticism Is Value

Your ads and funnels need to be valuable in themselves. Prove you can help people by actually helping them. Unpack a bit of your product. Give them a short bit of the results in the ad itself. When you do that, the prospect thinks: "If this person is giving this away for free, imagine what their paid stuff is like."

**Make your free stuff better than your competitor's paid stuff.** When you do that, you take over the market.

Your ads must directly attack skepticism head-on. Sell against all the other alternatives that exist in your marketplace. The question plaguing your prospect's mind is: **"How is this different?"**

A buyer is a buyer is a buyer. The people buying from you are buying from everybody else. Understand what they're buying so you can sell against those things.

### Handle Objections in the Copy

After writing your sales message, it should sound so compelling that only a lunatic wouldn't buy. If people still aren't buying, the answer is usually: they don't believe you.

Handle objections directly in the copy before they come up. Highlight: "This person had this problem, tried this, but once they tried your thing, they got all these benefits." As they read, they're nodding: "Yes, that makes sense." All objections handled. That's when people pull out their wallets.

## 15. Headlines: The Power of Attention-Grabbing Headlines

### The Story of "Astrological Love"

In 1982, Norah Hayden wrote a book called *Astrological Love*. It bombed — sold about 2,000 copies, the rest were dumped at dollar stores.

A New York publisher picked it up, bought the rights, and republished it. He didn't change a single word inside. He just changed the title and the front cover.

**New title:** *How to Satisfy a Woman Every Time and Have Her Beg for More*

**Result:** 2.3 million copies sold in the first 18 months. New York Times bestseller.

Same book. Same content. Different packaging. That is the power of a compelling headline.

As David Ogilvy said: once you've written your headline, you've already spent 80 cents of your dollar. Five times more people will read the headline than the rest of your body copy. Spend 80% of your time on the headline.

### Where to Study Headlines

Don't just study classic copywriters — they didn't compete with the chaotic blizzard of the internet. Study where the best headline writers are today: follow the money and the attention.

- TMZ, New York Times, Lad Bible, social media sites, gossip sites
- They are masters at hijacking attention
- They use: breaking news, "exposed," numbers, shocking images, 3–4 gripping words

You're not just competing with your local competitor anymore. You're on the internet, competing with everything on your prospect's news feed.

Study the most shared articles on Facebook and Instagram. The most visited websites in the world. Look at the copy they use. They use words like: exposed, shocked, leak, "wild details emerge." They elicit an emotion in the first 3 seconds.

Don't reinvent the wheel — swipe and deploy, making it applicable to your market.

### The 5 Headline Essentials

**1. It Must Grab Them**
It doesn't need to tell the full story. It needs to grab your prospect by the throat and demand their attention. Most headlines should be 3–4 words — a grabber. Think about the New York Times: "Shock Horror in New York." The headline's job is nothing more than to grab attention.

**2. Always Use Numbers**
Numbers give prospects a tangible object to wrap their mind around. That's why listicles work. "The 7 things no financial planner would dare tell you" — you immediately want to know what those 7 things are. Every time we've done a blind split test, headlines with numbers outperform those without.

**3. Have Burning Curiosity**
Load it up with curiosity. Use buzzwords: shocking, alarming, breaking news. Add a twist/qualifier: "7 things no property investor would dare tell you." Address the biggest objection: "How to start an online business, even if you're a complete beginner and have absolutely no idea where to start."

**4. Show What's In It For Them**
Every ad needs a big benefit. You can catch more flies with honey than vinegar. Always promise a big, specific benefit — that answers "What's in it for me?" Talk more about them than about you.

**5. Make Your Headlines Specific**
You can't say "grow your business." You need to say: "Here's how to get 150 to 250 highly qualified leads each and every month on autopilot." Or: "Here's how to lose 12–15 kgs in the next 9 weeks without eating like a rabbit or doing endless cardio." The more jaded your market, the deeper the specificity needed.

## 16. Timeless Headline Formulas

**Formula 1:** X ways to achieve [desirable thing] without doing [undesirable thing]
> *Example: "6 ways to get washboard abs without doing a single sit-up"*

**Formula 2:** Do [difficult thing] in [specific period of time], even if [shortcomings]
> *Example: "Pay off your mortgage in 7 years even if you have a modest income"*

**Formula 3:** Achieve [desired thing] like an expert, even without [something expected]
> *Example: "Paint a masterpiece like Picasso without ever having a single lesson"*

**Formula 4:** How to eliminate [biggest problem] without [the thing they hate] within [specific time frame]
> *Example: "How to eliminate your muffin top without giving up the foods you love within 21 days"*

## 17. Bullet Copy / Fascinations: The Secret Weapon

These little teaser nuggets of copy need to be hyper-specific and create enough intrigue that someone would buy off one bullet alone. Each bullet should justify the purchase all by itself.

Bullet copy is often one of the most-read parts of a sales message because it's scannable. People read the headline, sub-headline, scroll to the bottom, check the price and CTA, scroll back up, read sub-headlines, then spend disproportionate time reading bullets because they're small and digestible.

Don't use bullets to make specific claims. Don't barrage and sell. **Play with them. Tease them.** Dial curiosity up to 10/10.

As Claude Hopkins said: *"Curiosity is among the strongest human incentives."*

### Fascination Formulas

1. **You need X, right? Wrong.** — Address a common belief and talk against it.
   > *"You need to eat at a calorie deficit to lose weight. Wrong."*

2. **X ways to [why]**
   > *"5 ways to meet single women in Melbourne"*

3. **Where to find [why]**
   > *"Where to find the most ravenous, hyperactive buyers online"*

4. **How to eliminate X**
   > *"How to stop joint pain forever"*

5. **What you should never do** — People are more compelled to know what NOT to do (fear of loss)

6. **Say goodbye to X frustration**
   > *"Say goodbye to calorie counting and hour-long cardio sessions"*

7. **The truth about [why]**
   > *"The dirty truth about fish oil revealed"*
   > *"The dirty truth about property investment that no one would dare tell you"*

## 18. Long-Form Copy vs. Short-Form Copy

Only morons believe short-form copy will perform better than long-form copy — with one caveat: you can't have copy that's long for the sake of being long.

Someone who's not going to read long copy is also not going to read short copy. A buyer reads all the copy. They want every objection handled, every question answered.

Think about your red-hot pitch — typically 40 minutes. Your copy needs to replicate that. You can't do that in three paragraphs.

### But Edit Ruthlessly

Even though long-form converts better, the very first thing after writing is to **edit and cut**. Get it all out on paper, then ruthlessly edit. Every single word needs to fight for its right to be on the page. Each word must move the prospect one step closer to buying. It can't ramble.

### My Book Funnel Example

My book funnel landing page is 8,000–12,000 words. It didn't start there. I kept making it longer, and every time, conversion rate went up, AOV went up, ROAS went up.

### Make It "Read or Die"

It must be so interesting the reader feels like they *have* to keep reading. It needs to be entertaining. Use simple words, short sentences, but also wink at your prospect and add elements of entertainment. Make it fun — not a task.

### Who Reads All This Copy?

The buyers. The people who have questions that need answering. They've tried multiple other products. They read every word, the Q&A, the reviews — they're scouring the depths of the internet.

Don't show your copy to friends who aren't in the market. Show it to actual prospects.
